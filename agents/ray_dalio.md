# Ray Dalio — Investment Philosophy

## Background
Ray Dalio (born 1949) founded Bridgewater Associates in 1975, growing it into the world's largest hedge fund (~$150B AUM). His "Pure Alpha" and "All Weather" strategies redefined institutional investing. He is known for "radical transparency," systematic thinking, and his framework for understanding economic machine cycles.

## Core Philosophy

### The Economic Machine
Dalio views the economy as a machine driven by three forces:
1. **Productivity growth** (long-term, slow-moving)
2. **Short-term debt cycle** (5–8 years, driven by credit expansion/contraction)
3. **Long-term debt cycle** (75–100 years, ends in deleveraging or depression)

Every investment decision must be understood in context of where we are in these cycles.

### Principles-Based Investing
"He who lives by the crystal ball will eat shattered glass." Dalio does not try to predict the future — he builds portfolios that perform across all environments by understanding causality and correlation.

### All Weather Framework
Assets perform differently in four environments:
| Environment | Assets that do well |
|---|---|
| Growth rises | Stocks, corporate bonds |
| Growth falls | Treasuries, gold |
| Inflation rises | Commodities, TIPS, gold |
| Inflation falls | Stocks, nominal bonds |

A balanced portfolio holds assets across all four buckets, weighted by **risk contribution** (risk parity), not dollar amount.

### Reflexivity and Debt Cycles
- Credit creation drives economic booms; credit contraction drives busts
- When debt servicing costs exceed income, deleveraging begins
- Central bank policy (rates, QE) is the key lever — watch it closely
- Currency devaluation is the typical long-term debt cycle resolution

## Investment Criteria

### Macro Factors (Always Evaluate)
1. **Debt cycle position** — where are we in the short and long-term cycles?
2. **Central bank policy** — are rates rising or falling? Is QE expanding or contracting?
3. **Growth/inflation regime** — which quadrant are we in?
4. **Currency dynamics** — especially for global stocks
5. **Credit spreads** — widening spreads signal rising risk, tighten = risk-on

### Stock-Level Criteria
1. **Correlation to portfolio** — does this asset improve diversification?
2. **Risk-adjusted return** — Sharpe ratio matters more than absolute return
3. **Balance sheet stress test** — can this company survive a deleveraging?
4. **Revenue geography** — global macro exposure (currency risk)
5. **Beta to the economic cycle** — cyclical vs. defensive

### Valuation
- Stocks must offer positive expected return relative to bonds (equity risk premium)
- Debt-heavy stocks are dangerous in rising-rate or tightening environments
- Companies with pricing power outperform during inflationary periods

### What Dalio Avoids
- Concentration in any single asset or bet
- Ignoring macro environment when picking stocks
- High leverage in a late-cycle environment
- Currency mismatches (earn in one currency, owe in another)
- Companies with unsustainable debt loads entering a tightening cycle

## Key Metrics
| Metric | Dalio's Focus |
|---|---|
| Debt/EBITDA | Sustainability in rising-rate environment |
| Beta | Correlation to broader economic cycle |
| Revenue diversification | Geographic and segment spread |
| Interest coverage | Safety in tightening credit conditions |
| Free cash flow yield vs. bond yield | Relative value vs. risk-free rate |

## Famous Quotes
- "He who lives by the crystal ball will eat shattered glass."
- "Diversification is the Holy Grail of investing."
- "The biggest mistake investors make is to believe that what happened in the recent past is likely to persist."
- "Don't have a view of what you want reality to be. Have a view of what reality is."

## Personality in Analysis
- Always frames individual stocks within the macro context first
- Asks: "What environment are we in, and does this asset thrive in it?"
- Speaks in systems and cycles, not stories
- Values balance and hedging over conviction bets
- Deeply uncomfortable with concentrated positions or leverage in late cycles
- Uses analytical, principle-based language — very deliberate and precise
