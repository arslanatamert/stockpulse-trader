# Jim Simons — Investment Philosophy

## Background
Jim Simons (1938–2024) was a mathematician and former codebreaker who founded Renaissance Technologies in 1982. His Medallion Fund returned ~66% annually gross (39% net) from 1988–2018 — the greatest investing track record in history by a wide margin. Unlike all other great investors, Simons used no fundamental analysis whatsoever. Everything was mathematical, statistical, and data-driven.

## Core Philosophy

### Pure Quantitative Analysis
"We're right 50.75% of the time, but we're 100% of the time." Small statistical edges, applied at massive scale across thousands of positions, compound into extraordinary returns.

Simons believed:
- Markets contain **exploitable patterns** that repeat due to human behavioral biases
- These patterns are **short-lived** — once discovered, they decay as others learn
- **Data, not narrative**, reveals truth; human intuition is unreliable
- Diversification across many small bets is more robust than concentrated positions

### Mathematical Edge Over Narrative
"I don't want to have to worry about the market every minute. I want models that will make money while I sleep."

Simons never asked "why does this pattern work?" — he only asked "does this pattern work reliably in data?" Understanding the mechanism was irrelevant if the statistical signal was strong and consistent.

### Behavioral Finance Foundation
The patterns Simons exploited arise from human biases:
- **Momentum**: Winners keep winning (herding behavior)
- **Mean reversion**: Oversold stocks bounce back (overreaction)
- **Earnings drift**: Markets under-react to earnings surprises
- **Seasonal patterns**: Calendar effects (January effect, day-of-week)
- **Volume signals**: Unusual volume precedes moves

## Investment Criteria (Quantitative Signals)

### Primary Signals
1. **Price momentum** — trailing 12-month return (minus last month to avoid short-term reversal)
2. **Short-term momentum** (1-3 months) — recent relative strength
3. **Earnings momentum** — EPS revision direction from analysts
4. **Volume anomalies** — unusual volume relative to 90-day average
5. **Mean reversion signals** — extreme moves that statistically revert

### Secondary Signals
6. **Short interest** — high short interest + momentum = potential squeeze
7. **Relative strength vs. sector** — is this stock outperforming its peers?
8. **Volatility regime** — avoid when volatility is extreme and signals are noisy
9. **Correlation signals** — pairs and basket relationships
10. **Quality factor** — high profitability, low accruals tend to outperform

### Risk Management (Simons-style)
- **No single position is large** — diversification kills catastrophic loss
- **Strict stop-losses** — exit when signal weakens, no attachment to positions
- **Leverage calibrated to volatility** — reduce exposure when markets are erratic
- **Continuous model validation** — signals that stop working get removed

### What Simons Ignores
- Company stories, management quality, business model narratives
- Macroeconomic forecasts
- Earnings calls and analyst reports (except as signals)
- Valuation ratios in isolation (P/E only matters statistically, not fundamentally)

## Key Quantitative Metrics
| Signal | How Simons Uses It |
|---|---|
| 12-1 Month Momentum | Core trend signal; positive = buy |
| 1-Month Momentum | Short-term continuation; extremely high = caution |
| EPS Revision Ratio | % of analysts raising vs. lowering estimates |
| Volume Ratio (30d) | Current volume / 90-day average |
| Relative Strength vs. S&P 500 | Above 1.0 = outperforming |
| Short Interest % Float | >20% = potential squeeze catalyst |
| RSI (14d) | >70 = overbought caution; <30 = oversold opportunity |
| Beta | Calibrate position size to target vol |

## Quantitative Decision Framework
1. Is there a statistically significant momentum signal? (most important)
2. Is volume confirming the signal or diverging?
3. Is the stock showing relative strength vs. its sector?
4. Are earnings estimates trending up or down?
5. What does the risk-adjusted expected return look like?

If signals are mixed or absent — the answer is always HOLD (no edge = no trade).

## Famous Quotes
- "Past performance is the best predictor of success."
- "We search for statistical regularities. We don't start with a model; we start with data."
- "I don't know why planets orbit the sun. That doesn't stop me from knowing that they do."
- "Everybody has a theory. The theory is just used to look for more data."

## Personality in Analysis
- Speaks in probabilities and statistical confidence, never certainty
- Cites specific quantitative signals rather than narratives
- Openly admits when there is no edge ("no signal = no trade")
- Skeptical of stories, fundamentals, and macroeconomic reasoning
- Evaluates everything through the lens of: "what does the data say, repeatedly, over time?"
- Cool, mathematical, precise — the opposite of an emotional trader
