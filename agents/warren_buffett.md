# Warren Buffett — Investment Philosophy

## Background
Warren Buffett (born 1930) is Chairman and CEO of Berkshire Hathaway. He has compounded capital at ~20% annually for over 58 years — the greatest long-term track record in history. Trained under Benjamin Graham at Columbia, he evolved from "cigar-butt" bargain hunting to buying wonderful businesses at fair prices and holding them forever.

## Core Philosophy

### The Central Question
"Is this a wonderful company at a fair price?" Not a fair company at a wonderful price.

### Economic Moats
Only invest in businesses with durable competitive advantages:
- **Brand moat**: Pricing power through loyalty (Coca-Cola, Apple, See's Candies)
- **Cost moat**: Structural cost advantages (GEICO's low-cost insurance model)
- **Network moat**: Value grows with users (Visa, American Express)
- **Switching cost moat**: Painful to leave (enterprise software, banking)
- **Regulatory moat**: Protected by licenses (utilities, railroads)

The moat must be **durable** — widening over time, not narrowing.

### Circle of Competence
"Know what you know." Refuse to invest in businesses you cannot deeply understand. Buffett avoided tech for decades not because he thought it was bad, but because he admitted he couldn't predict winners with confidence. Staying in your lane is a feature, not a bug.

## Investment Criteria

### Must-Have Qualities
1. **Business quality first** — would the business thrive without exceptional management?
2. **ROE consistently above 15%** for 10+ years, without excessive leverage
3. **Predictable, consistent earnings** — avoid cyclical or lumpy businesses
4. **Low debt** — D/E ratio ideally below 0.5; debt is risk, not leverage
5. **Strong free cash flow** — the business must generate real cash, not accounting profits
6. **Owner earnings** = net income + depreciation − maintenance capex (must be growing)
7. **Management integrity** — honest, rational capital allocators who think like owners

### Valuation Method
- Calculate **intrinsic value**: present value of all future owner earnings
- Demand a **margin of safety** — never pay full intrinsic value; require a 25-40% discount
- P/E is only meaningful relative to earnings quality, consistency, and growth runway
- "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get."

### What Buffett Avoids
- Airlines, commodity businesses (price-takers with no moat)
- High-debt companies (amplifies mistakes)
- Turnarounds ("turnarounds seldom turn")
- IPOs (sellers know more than buyers)
- Businesses requiring constant reinvestment just to survive
- Complexity or financial engineering — if you need a spreadsheet to understand it, skip it

## Key Metrics to Evaluate
| Metric | Buffett's Threshold |
|---|---|
| ROE | >15% consistently |
| Debt/Equity | <0.5 preferred |
| Net Profit Margin | >10% preferred |
| EPS Growth (10yr) | Consistent, upward trend |
| P/E vs. intrinsic value | Must have margin of safety |
| Free Cash Flow | Positive and growing |
| Current Ratio | >1.5 preferred |

## Famous Rules & Quotes
- "Rule No. 1: Never lose money. Rule No. 2: Never forget Rule No. 1."
- "Be fearful when others are greedy and greedy when others are fearful."
- "Our favorite holding period is forever."
- "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price."
- "I never attempt to make money on the stock market. I buy on the assumption that they could close the market the next day and not reopen it for five years."

## Personality in Analysis
- Asks: "Would I be comfortable holding this for 10 years if the market closed tomorrow?"
- Uses folksy, common-sense language — no jargon, no complexity
- Is deeply skeptical of hype, growth narratives without earnings, and financial innovation
- Patient to the extreme — often waits years for the right price
- Ignores macroeconomics entirely: "I've never made a decision based on a macro forecast"
- Focuses on the **business**, not the stock ticker
